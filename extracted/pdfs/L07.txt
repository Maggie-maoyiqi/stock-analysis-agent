7 Classification Models
7.1 Introduction
In regression analyses, the response variable Y is related to some continuous explanatory
variables, while in practice, Y may be related to qualitative (or nominal, categorical) explana-
tory variables such as gender, race, machine type, categorized age, geographic location and
so on. A study of effects of such variables, sometimes called factors, on the response variable
entails a special form of ANOVA.
A level of a factor is a particular form of that factor. For example:
Factor Levels No. of levels
Gender Male, female 2
Race Oriental, Caucasian, African 3
Age <12, 12–21, 22–40, 41–65, >65 5
Region Urban, rural, industrial 3
A treatment is a particular combination of factor levels. In a single-factor study, a treat-
ment is simply a particular level of the factor. For example:
(i) one factor: gender.
no. of levels: 2 (male & female)
no. of possible treatments: 2 (male & female)
(ii) two factors: gender & race.
no. of levels for gender: 2 (male & female)
no. of levels for race: 3 (Oriental, Caucasian, African)
no. of possible treatments: 2 ×3= 6 (Oriental male, Oriental female, Caucasian male,
Caucasian female, African male, African female)
Main objective:
to determine whether the treatment groups have any systematic effects on the re-
sponse variable, and to examine the nature of these effects.
Technically, our underlying model treats all observations belonging to the same treatment
group as a random sample from the same normal distribution (observations are more homo-
geneous within each group). The idea is to study how the means of all these normal distribu-
tions vary over the different treatment groups.
Special case:
Two treatment groups−→two-sample problem for normal random samples (useful tools:
two-sample t -test and confidence interval based on Student’s t distributions).

Tests for main effects are usually performed when the test for interactions reveals an in-
significant result. There are two ways to proceed. (1) Under full model: The tests are as given
in the two-way ANOVA table on p17. (2) Under additive model: Merge SSAB into SSE, i.e.
SSAB + SSE −→SSE with d f = (I−1)( J−1) + I J (r−1) = I J r−I−J + 1. Testing for main effects
of A and main effects of B can be constructed accordingly.
If there is strong evidence for the existence of interactions, studying the main effects may
not be meaningful since these effects vary over different levels of the other factor. Aggregating
these effects over all levels of the other factor conceals the reality.
