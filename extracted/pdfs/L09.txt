9 Extraneous Variation
9.1 Introduction
Studies of classification models sometimes encounter an extraneous source of variation, which
may affect the responses or mask the true treatment effects, but is itself not of our primary
interest.
Examples:
(i) Responses to different treatments are observed on experimental units located at differ-
ent regions. ‘Region’ is an extraneous variable.
(ii) Responses to different treatments are observed across a certain period of time. ‘Time’
is an extraneous variable.
(iii) Quality tests are done on products manufactured by different production lines. ‘Pro-
duction line’ is an extraneous variable.
Subjects having similar attributes of the extraneous variable usually exhibit more similar
or homogeneous behaviour than subjects having different attributes of that variable. If it is
not possible to remove extraneous variables, it is at least desirable to control them so as to
• reduce experimental error variance, i.e. decrease the error S.S.,
• increase precision of experiment, i.e. increase power to detect treatment effects.
A common way to control extraneous variation is to incorporate any extraneous variables
into the classification model in the form of additive effects.
Type of extraneous variable Terminology Model
Categorical blocking factor or blocks
randomized block design
Continuous concomitant variable or covariate
analysis of covariance (ANCOVA)
An important assumption underlying the randomized block design or ANCOVA is that the
extraneous variables do not interact with the treatment effects. Thus,
• for randomized block design, comparisons between treatment effects are unaffected by
the block within which such comparisons are made;
• for ANCOVA, comparisons between treatment effects are unaffected by the values of
concomitant variables at which such comparisons are made.

9.4 Analysis of covariance (ANCOVA)
ANCOVA combines features of both regression and classification models.
In an ANCOVA model, additive effects of one or more additional quantitative extraneous vari-
ables are introduced to augment a multiway classification model in order to reduce variation
in the responses systematically. The added variables, usually known as concomitant variables
or covariates, account for extraneous variation which may not be of our primary interest.
Examples of concomitant variables:
• Locations of experimental units measured by their altitude;
• Time, age, years spent on education, IQ, weight, aptitude etc.;
• Pre-study attitudes of respondents, pre-study sales etc.
