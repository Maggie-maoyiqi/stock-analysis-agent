10.2 Identifying unusual observations
Outlying observations
Outlier is an observation that is well separated from the rest of the data. Outliers are sam-
ple observations which have unusual values for the response variable Y . More specifically, if
ห
the response Yi deviates considerably more from its fitted value
Yi than other sample values
deviate from their fitted values, we say that sample observation i is an outlier.
It is important to spot outliers among the data and determine whether they are simply
there by chance. If the answer is no, then it is of the experimenterโs interest to check whether
the outliers result from a mistake or other extraneous effects, and hence should be discarded.
A major reason for discarding them is that under the least squares method, a fitted line may
be pulled disproportionately toward an outlying observation and could cause a misleading
fit.
Outliers may also arise from atypical values of explanatory variables for which the model
does not give an adequate representation. Here the model should be reexamined to deter-
mine the range of values of explanatory variables for which it is adequate. Observations out-
side this range may be analyzed separately, or a better model should be constructed.

Handling outliers and influential observations
There are many reasons that a particular observation is found to be an outlier or influ-
ential observation. For example, recording error or the working model is incorrect. It is
recommended to recheck the data first.
If outlying or influential observations are obtained in some exceptional rare events which
will be very unlikely to occur in the future, then dropping these observations may be ap-
propriate. For example, HSI in October, 1987 (1987 stock market crash).
If no explanation can be found for the occurrence of outliers or influential observations,
there are some methods to deal with this problem:
(a) use other models with other transformations on Y or X ,
(b) (c) add dummy variables to remove the effect due to these observations,
drop these observations if the above two methods do not help,
(d) robust methods.
On the other hand, outliers may convey significant information if, for example, they are
caused by the interaction with an omitted explanatory variable.
It is recommended to discard an outlier only if there is direct evidence that it represents
an error in recording, a miscalculation, a malfunctioning of equipment, or a similar type
of circumstances.
